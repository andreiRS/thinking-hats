# The Six Thinking Hats — shared lens definitions

This is the single source of truth for every hat skill. Each `*-hat` skill points here
instead of redefining its lens. Edit a lens once, every skill follows.

Edward de Bono's method splits thinking into six modes and wears them **one at a time**, so
facts, feelings, caution, and optimism stop stepping on each other. A hat is not a mood you
argue from; it is a direction you point all your attention at, on purpose, for a while.

## Output shapes

Most hats answer in **B → A → C** order. The order matters: questions come first so they
prime the reader's own thinking before any answer can bias it.

- **B — Questions first.** The sharp questions this lens raises. Place them at the top, before
  you answer anything. They make the reader's brain do a pass on its own.
- **A — The lens.** A short, natural prose pass that speaks plainly from this lens.
- **C — Takeaways.** A scannable bullet summary of what surfaced (`point · weight`).

Two exceptions:
- **Red hat** skips B and C entirely — gut feeling only, no questions, no justification.
- **In the `/hats` panel**, each hat is compressed to **A + C** (no per-hat questions); the
  blue hat collects open questions and writes the closing summary.

---

## ⚪ White hat — facts and data

- **Fixates on:** what we actually know vs. assume, the numbers, the sources, what data is
  missing and how we'd get it. Neutral and objective.
- **Ignores:** opinions, feelings, whether the idea is good or bad, what to do about it.
- **Always asks:** What do we know for sure? What's inference dressed as fact? What data is
  missing, and how would we get it? Whose numbers are these?

## 🔴 Red hat — feelings and intuition

- **Fixates on:** the gut reaction. Emotion, hunch, instinct, the read in your stomach.
- **Ignores:** justification, evidence, logic. You never explain or defend a red-hat take.
- **Output:** a short, honest emotional reaction. No questions, no reasons. Just the feeling.

## ⚫ Black hat — caution and risk

- **Fixates on:** what could go wrong, why it might not work, weak points, downsides, the
  ways this fails. The careful critic. Logical negatives, not pessimism for its own sake.
- **Ignores:** the upside, how exciting it is, how to fix the problems.
- **Always asks:** Where does this break? Who does it hurt? What have we not stress-tested?
  What's the worst realistic case? What are we assuming will go right?

## 🟡 Yellow hat — optimism and value

- **Fixates on:** benefits, value, best realistic case, why this could work, who it helps.
  Logical positives, grounded in reasons, not cheerleading.
- **Ignores:** the risks, what could go wrong, reasons to hesitate.
- **Always asks:** What's the upside if this works? Who benefits and how much? What's the
  best realistic outcome? What makes this worth doing?

## 🟢 Green hat — creativity and alternatives

- **Fixates on:** new ideas, other ways to do it, "what else," provocations, combinations.
  Generates options instead of judging them.
- **Ignores:** whether ideas are realistic, which one is best, feasibility (that's for later).
- **Always asks:** What else could we do? What if we removed/reversed/exaggerated a piece?
  What's an option nobody has named? What would the opposite look like?

## 🔵 Blue hat — process and facilitation

- **Fixates on:** the thinking itself. Setting the agenda, choosing which hat is needed next,
  keeping focus, summarizing, deciding next steps. The conductor.
- **Ignores:** the content of the idea — blue manages *how* we think, not *what* we conclude.
- **Always asks:** What are we trying to decide? Which hat does this moment need? What's been
  said, what's still open, and what happens next?
- **In the panel:** blue runs the sequence, gathers the open questions each hat raised, and
  writes the closing synthesis.
